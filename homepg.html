Pattachitra Art

Art of Orissa & West Bengal

Pattachitra Art — Cloth Painting rooted in mythology


Origin : Ancient scroll painting from Odisha & West Bengal; name means “picture on cloth”

Themes : Hindu mythology — especially stories of Lord Jagannath, Krishna, Rama, and epics like the Ramayana

Style : Bold outlines, vibrant natural colors, detailed floral borders, and symmetrical designs

Medium : Hand-painted on cloth using natural dyes, with handmade brushes from animal hair

Special Forms : Also made on palm leaves (etched) in Odisha

Cultural Role : Deeply tied to rituals at Puri’s Jagannath Temple — a sacred offering, not just art

Chola Bronzes

Sculpture Craving of Tamil Nadu.

Chola Bronzes — Divine Grace Cast in Metal

Origin : Tamil Nadu, during the Chola Dynasty (9th–13th century), especially in Thanjavur and Kumbakonam regions

Themes : Hindu deities such as Shiva (as Nataraja), Vishnu, Parvati, Lakshmi, and saints like the Nayanmars

Style : Elegant, balanced forms with fluid body postures and serene facial expressions; symbolic gestures (*mudras*)

Technique : Lost-wax casting (cire-perdue) — a meticulous metal casting method that produces one-of-a-kind bronze idols

Medium : Panchaloha — an alloy of five metals: gold, silver, copper, zinc, and iron

Significance : Considered sacred and used in temple rituals; the Nataraja symbolizes cosmic dance, creation, and destruction

Warli Art

Folk painting from Maharastra.

Warli Art — Tribal tales in white on mud walls


Origin : Indigenous to the Warli tribe of Maharashtra; dates back to 2500 BCE

Style : Simple geometric shapes — circles, triangles, and lines — used to depict daily life, farming, dance, animals, and festivals

Medium : Traditionally painted in white rice paste on red ochre (mud) walls

Visual Identity : Minimalist, symbolic, and rhythmic — often shows community life and nature

Cultural Role : Used to celebrate harvests, weddings, and rituals; not decorative, but deeply spiritual

Modern Form : Now seen on canvases, fabrics, walls, and even global fashion and branding

Madhubani Art

Folk painting from Bihar.

Madhubani Art — Mythology painted in vibrant symmetry

Origin : From the Mithila region of Bihar; traditionally practiced by women on walls during festivals and rituals

Themes : Gods and goddesses like Krishna, Ram, Durga, nature, love, and auspicious symbols

Style : Highly decorative, symmetric, with double lines, intricate patterns, and no blank space

Medium : Painted on cloth, handmade paper, or walls using natural dyes and twigs or fingers

Forms : Includes Bharni, Kachni, Tantrik, and Godna styles

Significance : More than art — it's a sacred expression of life, fertility, and celebration

Khajuraho Stone Carvings

Sculpture craving of Madhya Pradesh

Khajuraho Sculptures — Carved Temples of Spirituality and Life

Origin : Chandela Dynasty, 10th–11th century, Khajuraho (UNESCO World Heritage Site), Madhya Pradesh

Themes : Deities, apsaras (celestial maidens), musicians, mythical creatures, daily life, and symbolic eroticism

Style : Highly detailed sandstone carvings in both narrative and symbolic styles, covering outer temple walls

Medium : Stone carving with hammer and chisel; emphasis on rhythm, balance, and realistic expression

Significance : Represents spiritual union, harmony of body and soul, and cultural inclusivity of medieval India

Kalamkari Art

Folk painting from Andhra Pradesh & Telangana .

Kalamkari Art — Storytelling with a pen and natural dyes

Origin : From the Persian words “kalam” (pen) and “kari” (craft), this hand-painted or block-printed art is native to Andhra Pradesh and Telangana

Style : Mythological scenes from Ramayana, Mahabharata, and epic lore — drawn with intricate detailing

Technique : Done using a bamboo or date-palm stick nib; 20+ steps including natural dyeing, bleaching, hand painting, and washing

Types :

Srikalahasti (hand-painted with kalam)

Machilipatnam (block-printed with vegetable dyes)

Medium : Painted on cotton/silk fabric using natural dyes from plants, roots, and minerals

Use Today : Widely seen on sarees, wall hangings, dupattas, and home decor — blending tradition with fashion
